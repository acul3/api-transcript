Case: Congestion & Fever - 2-month-old
Specialty: Pediatrics - Neonatal
Description: A 2-month-old female with 1-week history of congestion and fever x2 days.
Word Count: 788
============================================================

Doctor: Hi, is this Mrs. Thompson?

Patient: Yes, this is her! 

Doctor: Hi, this is Dr. Davis calling from the Pediatric Clinic. How are you doing today?

Patient: Oh, I’m a bit worried actually. My baby girl has been sick for about a week now.

Doctor: I'm sorry to hear that. Can you tell me a little more about her symptoms?

Patient: Sure. She’s two months old and she’s had this congestion and a fever for the last two days. Her temperature has been as high as 104 at times.

Doctor: That does sound concerning. Has she been coughing as well?

Patient: Yes, she has. The cough is pretty bad, like... um, sometimes it really makes her turn red and she even vomits afterward, which is really alarming.

Doctor: I understand that must be very stressful for you. How long has she been having this cough?

Patient: It's been about a week, but it’s gotten worse over the last couple of days. Before, it was just a little congested, but now it’s more intense.

Doctor: Okay. And how is she doing with her feeding and diaper output? 

Patient: Not great, to be honest. She doesn’t want to eat as much—only about four ounces of Similac every two to three hours, and I've noticed fewer wet diapers. 

Doctor: That’s important to note, especially since decreased intake can lead to dehydration. I see you mentioned she had some irregular breathing too. Can you describe that a bit more?

Patient: Yes. Sometimes it seems like she’s breathing faster than usual? And I’ve seen her ribs pulling in a little when she breathes, like there’s some effort involved.

Doctor: Thanks for sharing that. It sounds like she may be experiencing some mild respiratory distress. We really need to keep an eye on that. 

Patient: Oh gosh, what does that mean for her?

Doctor: Well, given her symptoms—fever up to 104, the cough, and the breathing difficulties—we should definitely evaluate her further. We’ll be looking out for signs of sepsis, pneumonia, or even a urinary tract infection with some lab work.

Patient: Lab work? What kind of tests are you talking about?

Doctor: I’m planning on doing a few things. We’ll send out a pertussis PCR test since her cough has been paroxysmal, and I want to follow up on the urine culture because her UA showed some bacteria. 

Patient: Oh, I see. 

Doctor: Yes. And for the immediate plan, we’re admitting her to the pediatric floor for closer monitoring. 

Patient: Admission? Is that really necessary?

Doctor: Given her symptoms and the fact that she’s had decreased oral intake and wet diapers, it’s just a precaution to ensure she gets the right care. I know it sounds alarming, but we’ll monitor her very closely. 

Patient: Okay, I trust you. What about treatment? Is she going to need any medications?

Doctor: Yes, we’ll start by managing her fever. I recommend switching from ibuprofen to acetaminophen for now. She’ll be receiving supportive care, and if her breathing worsens, we might need to consider bronchodilators.

Patient: So how often will she need the acetaminophen?

Doctor: For her age, it’s usually 10 to 15 mg per kilogram, so we’ll start with a dose every six hours as needed. Just make sure to measure it out carefully.

Patient: Got it. I’ll keep track of that. And what about follow-up once she’s released?

Doctor: After she’s stabilized, I’d say let's schedule a follow-up appointment in about a week. We can reassess her symptoms and make sure everything is improving.

Patient: That works for me. 

Doctor: Great! Also, please keep an eye on her breathing. If you notice any worsening, or if she has any chest pain or shortness of breath, call emergency services immediately.

Patient: Oh, that sounds serious. I’ll be sure to watch for that.

Doctor: Mhmm, it’s always better to be safe. And try to keep her in a comfortable position, maybe even elevate her slightly while she sleeps.

Patient: Sure, I’ll do that. Is there anything else I should be aware of?

Doctor: Just keep a record of her fever and any cough episodes. And try to maintain her feeding schedule as best as you can.

Patient: Okay, I’ll make a log. 

Doctor: Alright, to recap: we’re admitting her for monitoring, switching to acetaminophen every six hours, keeping an eye on breathing, scheduling a follow-up in a week, and monitoring her symptoms closely.

Patient: Yes, thank you, Dr. Davis. I really appreciate it.

Doctor: Of course, Mrs. Thompson. We’re here to help. You can pick up any prescriptions we might need to send to the pharmacy once she’s settled. 

Patient: Thank you so much!

Doctor: You're welcome! Take care, and I’ll see you soon.

Patient: Bye!
