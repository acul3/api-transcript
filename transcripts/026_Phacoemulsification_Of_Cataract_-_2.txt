Case: Phacoemulsification Of Cataract - 2
Specialty: Ophthalmology
Description: Cataract, right eye.  Phacoemulsification of cataract with posterior chamber intraocular lens, right eye.
Word Count: 1662
============================================================

Doctor: Hi, this is Dr. Patel calling from the eye surgery center. Can you hear me okay?

Patient: Yes, hi, I can hear you. Sorry, I was just sitting down. Is this about my surgery today?

Doctor: It is, yes. I wanted to check in after your cataract surgery on your right eye and go over how things went and what to expect over the next few days.

Patient: Oh, thank goodness. I’ve been a little nervous. My eye feels kind of cloudy, but not painful.

Doctor: That’s very common right after surgery. You had phacoemulsification of the cataract in the right eye with placement of a posterior chamber intraocular lens. In simple terms, we removed the cloudy natural lens and put in a clear artificial lens inside the eye. The surgery went very well.

Patient: Okay… phaco… what did you call it?

Doctor: Phacoemulsification. It’s the technique we use to break up the cataract with ultrasound and remove it through a tiny incision. Your new lens is an SN60WS lens, and it was placed in the capsular bag where your natural lens used to sit. It’s centered well.

Patient: That’s a relief. So the cataract was definitely the problem?

Doctor: Yes. Your preoperative and postoperative diagnosis were both cataract, right eye. Based on your symptoms and exam, the lens had become cloudy enough to affect your vision. You had mentioned at your last visit that the blur had been getting worse over the past several months, especially with night driving and reading small print.

Patient: Right, yeah. I’ve been squinting all the time. And headlights at night were awful.

Doctor: Exactly. That fits very well with cataracts. You also had some glare and trouble seeing contrast. Were you having any pain before surgery?

Patient: No pain, just annoying blur. And I was worried because my left eye is okay, so I kept thinking maybe it was something more serious.

Doctor: I understand. The good news is this was a straightforward cataract surgery. There were no complications. Your vital signs stayed stable, and when we checked the eye pressure and the incision at the end, everything looked normal. No wound leak, the chamber stayed deep, and the eye was well sealed.

Patient: Okay, that sounds reassuring. I remember someone saying something about pressure in the eye?

Doctor: Yes, after surgery we check the anterior chamber pressure to make sure it’s in a safe range. Yours was normal by tactile check, meaning it felt appropriate, and the incision was hydrated properly so it sealed well.

Patient: Great. Um, I’m still a little blurry though. Is that supposed to happen?

Doctor: Yes, especially today. We put tetracaine 1% on the eye before surgery for numbing, and at the end we placed Maxitrol ointment and a tight patch with a Fox shield. So the blur you’re noticing now is expected from the ointment and the patching. Once you remove the patch tomorrow morning, vision may still be hazy at first but should gradually improve over the next several days.

Patient: Okay. I was worried something had gone wrong.

Doctor: No, nothing went wrong. You tolerated the procedure well and you had no eye pain in recovery. That’s a very good sign.

Patient: That’s good to hear. What do I need to do now with the eye drops? I don’t want to mess it up.

Doctor: Of course. Let me go over the plan carefully. Start your postoperative drops tomorrow after you remove the patch. You’ll use the antibiotic-steroid combination exactly as prescribed by the surgeon’s instructions. If you were given Maxitrol drops or ointment, use them as directed—usually the ointment at bedtime and the drops during the day, but follow the written schedule from discharge.

Patient: I think they gave me drops and ointment. I always get confused about the names.

Doctor: That’s okay. Maxitrol is spelled M-A-X-I-T-R-O-L. If there’s any doubt, check the bottle label. If you want, you can call the pharmacy or the office and we can confirm the directions line by line.

Patient: Thank you. I’m also on my blood pressure medicine and metformin. Do those matter with the eye drops?

Doctor: Good question. Your current medicines don’t interfere with the eye drops or the surgery itself. We always like to know about medical history, and I have that you have type 2 diabetes and hypertension. Diabetes can make us watch the eyes more closely because it can increase the risk of retinal problems over time, though your cataract surgery itself went smoothly. Your blood pressure has also been an important part of your overall health, but it was stable for this procedure.

Patient: Yeah, my sugar’s been okay lately. Last A1c was 7.4, I think.

Doctor: That sounds right from your chart. It’s not directly related to today’s surgery, but keeping that controlled helps protect your eyes long term. Are you still taking metformin 500 mg twice daily with meals?

Patient: Yes, 500 twice a day with breakfast and dinner. Sometimes I forget the evening one, to be honest.

Doctor: Try to keep that consistent. And if you’re having trouble remembering, a pill box or phone alarm can help. Also, after cataract surgery, it’s important not to rub the eye, even if it feels itchy.

Patient: Oh, I definitely feel like rubbing it right now.

Doctor: I know, but please don’t. The shield is there for a reason. Wear the Fox shield while sleeping for the first several nights, and avoid anything that could press on the eye.

Patient: Got it. Can I shower?

Doctor: Yes, but keep water and soap out of the eye for the first couple of days. No swimming, hot tubs, or heavy lifting over about 10 to 15 pounds for at least a week, unless your surgeon tells you otherwise.

Patient: Okay. I work part-time and I was supposed to help my daughter move boxes this weekend. Sounds like that’s a no?

Doctor: Correct, I’d avoid that. Pushing, bending, and lifting can increase pressure in the eye. If you can, let someone else handle the boxes.

Patient: [pause] This is probably a silly question, but when should I call if something feels wrong?

Doctor: Not silly at all. Call us right away or go to urgent care or the ER if you have severe eye pain, sudden worsening vision, a curtain-like shadow, lots of new floaters, flashes of light, increasing redness, pus-like discharge, or nausea with eye pain. Those can be warning signs of a more serious problem.

Patient: Okay. And what about follow-up?

Doctor: Your follow-up appointment should be in about one week, usually around 7 days after surgery, so we can check your healing, your eye pressure, and your vision. If you don’t already have the exact time, the front desk will call you. If not, we can get that scheduled before we hang up.

Patient: I think they said next Tuesday, but I wasn’t sure on the time.

Doctor: Let me see… yes, it looks like next Tuesday at 9:20 AM. Does that work for you?

Patient: Yes, that works. Should I bring anything?

Doctor: Just bring your drops, your medication list, and the shield if you have any questions about it. Also, it helps if you bring a list of any symptoms you’ve noticed, like pain, redness, or vision changes.

Patient: Okay. So just to make sure I understand, the cloudy lens is out and the new lens is in, and the blur now is from the surgery and ointment?

Doctor: That’s exactly right. The cataract in your right eye was removed, and the new intraocular lens is in place and centered well. The temporary blur is expected. Over the next few days, the eye should settle down and vision should improve gradually.

Patient: That makes me feel a lot better. I was imagining the worst.

Doctor: I understand. It’s normal to feel anxious after eye surgery. You did very well. One more thing—avoid driving today, and probably tomorrow as well, until your surgeon says your vision is safe for it.

Patient: Yeah, my husband is driving me around. He’ll be happy to hear that.

Doctor: Good. And keep using your regular medicines, including your metformin 500 mg twice daily, unless another doctor told you to stop something. If you take any new over-the-counter pain medicine, avoid anything unless you check with us first, especially if it’s not acetaminophen.

Patient: So I can take Tylenol if I need it?

Doctor: Yes, acetaminophen is usually fine for mild discomfort, as long as you stay within the recommended dosing on the package and don’t have liver disease. But honestly, most people don’t need much pain medicine after this procedure.

Patient: Okay. I don’t smoke or anything, but I do drink wine on weekends. Is that a problem?

Doctor: A small amount of alcohol is usually not an issue with the eye surgery itself, but tonight I’d suggest skipping it, especially if you’re taking any sedating medicine or if you’re just feeling tired from the procedure. Staying hydrated and resting is best.

Patient: All right. That all makes sense. I appreciate you calling.

Doctor: Of course. So just to recap what we discussed: you had successful cataract surgery on the right eye with placement of a posterior chamber lens, there were no complications, your eye pressure and incision looked normal, you should keep the patch and shield on as instructed, start the postoperative drops tomorrow, avoid rubbing the eye, heavy lifting, swimming, and driving for now, and come back next Tuesday at 9:20 AM for follow-up.

Patient: Perfect.

Doctor: And if you have severe pain, sudden vision loss, increasing redness, or discharge, call us right away or go to the ER. Do you have any other questions for me?

Patient: No, I think you covered everything. Thank you for explaining it in plain English.

Doctor: You’re very welcome. Take it easy today, and we’ll see you next Tuesday. Goodbye.

Patient: Goodbye, doctor. Thank you.
