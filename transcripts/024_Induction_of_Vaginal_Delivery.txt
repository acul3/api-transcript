Case: Induction of Vaginal Delivery
Specialty: Surgery
Description: Induction of vaginal delivery of viable male, Apgars 8 and 9.  Term pregnancy and oossible rupture of membranes, prolonged.
Word Count: 1620
============================================================

Doctor: Hi, this is Dr. Patel from the labor and delivery team calling to check in on you after your delivery yesterday. Can you hear me okay?

Patient: Yes, I can hear you. Hi, doctor. Uh, I was actually wondering if someone was going to call. I’m doing okay, just really tired.

Doctor: I’m glad you’re home and resting. First of all, congratulations on your baby boy. I wanted to go over what happened during your induction and make sure you’re feeling all right. How are you doing this morning?

Patient: Tired, sore, but relieved. Everything happened so fast. I keep replaying it in my head. Is the baby okay? They said his Apgars were good, but I don’t really know what that means.

Doctor: He is doing well. Yes, his Apgar scores were 8 at one minute and 9 at five minutes, which is excellent. That means right after birth he was vigorous, breathing well, moving all his extremities, and recovering quickly. He was born in the head-down position, occiput anterior, which is the ideal position, and there were no delivery complications.

Patient: Oh, thank God. I was so scared when they said I might have ruptured membranes. I didn’t even know what that meant exactly.

Doctor: Of course. Let me explain it simply. You came to the office because you had been having a small amount of leaking since the night before. On exam, your Nitrazine test was positive, which can suggest your water may have broken, but there was no ferning seen under the microscope, so it wasn’t totally definitive. Then on ultrasound your amniotic fluid index, or AFI, was about 4.7 centimeters, which is low. With the combination of the possible prolonged rupture of membranes, low fluid, and the cervix being not very favorable, we recommended induction.

Patient: Right, they said my cervix was “variable” or something. I didn’t understand that part either.

Doctor: Yes, your cervix wasn’t quite ready on its own, meaning it hadn’t softened and opened enough to expect labor to start efficiently. So we admitted you and started Pitocin to help the contractions get regular and strong enough.

Patient: That Pitocin made it really intense, wow. I remember once it kicked in, things moved so fast. Then you said you were going to break my water?

Doctor: Exactly. Once you were in a regular contraction pattern, we performed amniotomy, which means we ruptured the bag of waters. The fluid was clear, which is reassuring. After that, your labor progressed rapidly. You went to complete dilation in about an hour and a half, then you pushed for about 2 hours.

Patient: I can’t believe I pushed that long. I was exhausted. I thought something was wrong because it was taking so much effort.

Doctor: No, that was very normal, especially for your first delivery. You did very well. You delivered a viable baby boy vaginally, over an intact perineum, so you did not have any tears. Your estimated blood loss was about 150 mL, which is quite low and very good.

Patient: Okay, that’s a relief. I was worried about bleeding a lot. I’m still having some cramping though, and I’m sore down there, but not awful.

Doctor: Mild cramping after delivery is expected, especially as the uterus is shrinking back down. As long as the bleeding stays like a normal heavy period or less, and you’re not soaking through a pad every hour, that’s okay. Are you having any fever, chills, foul-smelling discharge, or increasing pain?

Patient: No fever. I’ve been checking because I was nervous about infection. The bleeding is more like a period, maybe a little heavier the first day, but not crazy. I’m just wiped out.

Doctor: That sounds reassuring. Since there was concern for prolonged rupture of membranes before delivery, we do always watch for infection, but your delivery went smoothly, the placenta came out spontaneously and intact, and there was no retained placenta. That lowers the risk of complications. Still, if you develop a temperature of 100.4 Fahrenheit or higher, bad-smelling vaginal discharge, worsening abdominal pain, or heavy bleeding, you should call us right away or go to the ER.

Patient: Okay. I remember they said the placenta was okay. What does “three-vessel cord” mean?

Doctor: Good question. The umbilical cord normally has three vessels: two arteries and one vein. Your baby’s cord had all three vessels, which is normal. It was delivered intact, and there was no concern for the placenta being left behind.

Patient: That’s good to hear. Uh, sorry, I’m kind of all over the place. I’m just still processing everything. Was there anything about my history that made this more likely?

Doctor: It’s okay, you can ask anything. Looking at your pregnancy history, you’re a 20-year-old gravida 4 para 0, meaning this was your fourth pregnancy and you had not delivered before this baby. With a first vaginal delivery, labor can sometimes be unpredictable and longer. The main risk factors in your case were the low fluid level, the possible rupture of membranes, and the fact that the cervix wasn’t favorable yet. Those were the reasons we felt induction was safest.

Patient: I see. So the low fluid, that AFI of 4.7, that was the big issue?

Doctor: Yes, along with the possible leaking. AFI around 4.7 centimeters is considered oligohydramnios, which means low amniotic fluid. That can sometimes be associated with membrane rupture or placental concerns, so we did the safer thing by inducing rather than waiting.

Patient: Makes sense. I’m glad I listened. I was honestly hoping I could just wait it out at home, but I guess not.

Doctor: You did the right thing by coming in. I also want to go over your postpartum plan. For pain, you can take acetaminophen 650 mg every 6 hours as needed, and ibuprofen 600 mg every 6 to 8 hours with food if you’re able to take NSAIDs. Don’t exceed the daily limits on either one. If you’re breastfeeding, both are generally compatible.

Patient: Okay. They gave me ibuprofen in the hospital, I think. Can I take both together?

Doctor: Yes, you can alternate them if needed. For example, acetaminophen first, then ibuprofen a few hours later. Just avoid taking other cold or flu medicines that also contain acetaminophen so you don’t accidentally double up.

Patient: Got it. I’m not on anything else right now. Well, besides the prenatal vitamins.

Doctor: Keep taking your prenatal vitamin for now, especially if you’re breastfeeding. Also, try to drink plenty of fluids and get small meals in when you can. Rest is important, but gentle walking around the house is good for circulation and helps prevent blood clots.

Patient: I’ve been mostly sitting with the baby. I’m scared to move too much because I’m so sore.

Doctor: Gentle movement is fine. You do not need exercise right away, but short walks in the house, like to the bathroom and back or around the room, are helpful. Avoid heavy lifting, and don’t put anything in the vagina until your postpartum visit, usually around 6 weeks.

Patient: Okay. What about sex or tampons? Sorry if that’s awkward.

Doctor: Not awkward at all. No tampons, no douching, and no intercourse until you’re cleared at your follow-up, typically at 6 weeks postpartum, or sooner if we need to see you for a wound check. Since you had no tears, healing should be straightforward, but we still want to wait until bleeding has stopped and the cervix has closed.

Patient: That helps. I’m also worried about mood stuff. I’ve heard about postpartum depression and I don’t want to miss anything.

Doctor: I’m glad you brought that up. After delivery, it’s common to have emotional changes, but if you feel persistently sad, hopeless, overwhelmed, unable to sleep even when the baby sleeps, or you have thoughts of harming yourself or the baby, call us immediately. Those can be signs of postpartum depression or another postpartum mood disorder.

Patient: Okay, I’ll watch for that. So what do I need to do next?

Doctor: So just to recap what we discussed: you had a term pregnancy with possible prolonged rupture of membranes and oligohydramnios, with an AFI of 4.7 centimeters, so we induced labor with Pitocin. You delivered a healthy baby boy vaginally, with Apgars of 8 and 9, clear fluid, intact perineum, no tears, and an estimated blood loss of 150 mL. At home, take acetaminophen 650 mg every 6 hours as needed and ibuprofen 600 mg every 6 to 8 hours with food if needed, keep taking your prenatal vitamins, stay hydrated, and walk lightly as tolerated. Watch for fever, heavy bleeding, foul-smelling discharge, severe pain, chest pain, shortness of breath, or mood symptoms. We’d like to see you back in about 6 weeks for your postpartum follow-up, sooner if anything concerns you.

Patient: Okay, that’s really clear. Thank you for explaining all of it. I was so out of it yesterday, I barely remember half of what happened.

Doctor: That’s very normal after labor. You did a great job. Do you have any other questions right now?

Patient: Um, just one. The baby is sleeping a lot. Is that normal?

Doctor: Yes, newborns sleep a lot in the first days. As long as he’s waking to feed, making wet diapers, and the pediatrician has no concerns, that’s expected. If he seems too hard to wake, isn’t feeding well, or has fewer wet diapers than expected, call the pediatrician.

Patient: Okay. I appreciate that. Thank you for calling.

Doctor: Of course. Congratulations again, and please call us if anything comes up. We’ll see you at your postpartum visit in about 6 weeks.

Patient: Thank you, doctor. Bye.

Doctor: Bye-bye, take care.
